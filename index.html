Lands End

My favorite park in San Francisco

A brief history of the park

What is now called Lands End was lived on and cared for by the Yelamu Ohlone tribe prior to Spanish colonisation.
When the Gold Rush came to San Francisco it changed the city dramatically. Buildings like the Cliffhouse and
Sutro Baths became part of this changing landscape on the outer edge of the city.
Adolph Sutro even constructed a passenger steam train from downtown to Lands Ends.
During this time a vein of bituminous coal was discovered on Sutro's land.
Sutro had a tunnel dug 200 feet under the railroad track and confirmed the find.
The remants of the train tracks and mine can still be seen in the park today.

Places to check out along the trails

There is a beautiful stone labrinth created by San Francisco Artist Eduardo Aguilera on an outcropping of land overlooking the sea.
The entrance is off a side trail at about the halfway point on the stretch of the main trail that wraps around the coast.
There is a wooden trail marker for eagles point that will greet you when you find the spot.
It is laid out on this beautiful vista and is worth the hike down some serious stairs to get there.

One of the most well known spots along this trail is the remains of Sutro Baths.
Created by Adolph Sutro in 1896. it was the largest indoor bath of its kind at the time.
A fire destroyed it in 1966, but remains of the site are still there, and easily accessed.
There are many passageways and a tunnel that you can walk through.
Make sure you are careful though, the site is in a state of deterioration and the concrete and metal that is exposed can be dangerous.

This next point of interest I have never explored. Although I am curious about it. As mentioned above, there is a coal mine long since abandoned on the parks property.
Getting there is no easy feat though, and only the most careful and experianced explorer could tackle it, I imagine.
The mine is located 200 feet down from the ominously named deadman's point that is really only accesible by treading water.
It is mostly eroded these days due to landslides and it's broken up into three different sections.

The Flora and Fauna

This park is wild in the best way. You will not see mowed grass or playgrounds here.
The plants and animals that reside here are mostly native and left to thrive.
Here are few inhabitants you might see in this beautiful place.

The Monterey Cypress is one of the most recognizable trees in the park and only found naturally on the central coast of California.
They have wildy twisted greyish brown wood and leaves that grow in dense sprays that are bright green, and have a citrusy aroma when crushed.
The twisting and flat topped look of the trees is the result of strong winds along the coastal range and create really incredible shapes.

Ruler of the skys, the Red Tailed Hawk. They are found through out San Francisco and stay here all year along.
The Raptors particularly love Sutro Heights and you can see them nesting in the trees facing the Cliff House.
They have a very hypnotic high pitched distinctive cry, and beautiful mottled reddish brown and white feathers.
